ChatGPT vs Perplexity vs Google AI — where should my D2C brand focus?

Focus on Perplexity for text visibility, Google AI for zero-click answers, and ChatGPT for deep entity recognition. Check them in that order to protect your D2C brand's future search presence.

Perplexity reads your page content directly. Google AI summarizes it instantly but rarely sends clicks. ChatGPT relies on structured data and sports databases that can misclassify you. Why do these engines give me opposite answers?

Perplexity described Yauhen Massalski correctly as helping D2C brands with AI-search and growth. It pulled from text it could read on my site.

Which engine sends real traffic?

Perplexity is the only one that sends clicks right now. It functions as a research tool. Users want sources. They click through to verify. If your page has clear, readable text, it wins here.

Google AI generates answers directly in the SERP. It is zero-click by design. It satisfies the user without sending them to your site. This is good for brand awareness. It is bad for direct traffic if you rely on clicks.

ChatGPT is a closed loop. It does not send traffic. It builds authority. If it knows who you are, you win long-term. You do not win immediate clicks.

Where should I start my audit?

Start with Perplexity. It is the easiest to control. You can test your own pages instantly. Ask it a question about your product. See if it cites your URL. If it does, you are visible. If it doesn't, your text is not clear enough.

Move to Google AI next. Check if your brand appears in the generated summaries. This is harder to control. You need strong entity signals across the web.

Check ChatGPT last. It requires deep entity work. You need consistent mentions in structured data. It takes months to shift. Perplexity moves in days.

How do I track each engine?

You cannot use standard analytics for all of them. Google AI traffic often gets buried in "direct" or "organic" without clear parameters. Perplexity sends referrers you can see. ChatGPT sends almost nothing.

Track visibility, not just clicks. Use a simple list. Check your brand name weekly. Note which engine cites you. Note which one ignores you. This is your real metric.

Why does ChatGPT still think I'm only an athlete?

ChatGPT relies on structured data sources. It trusts databases that label people by profession. If those databases say "athlete," it says "athlete." It ignores the nuance of side careers unless they are heavily cited in its training data.

Perplexity reads live web pages. It sees what is written now. It updates faster. It does not rely on static labels. This is why it got my description right.

How do I make AI answers recommend my brand?

You need to feed the right signal to the right engine. For Perplexity, write clear, dense text on your pages. Answer the specific questions buyers ask. For Google AI, build authority through citations and consistent branding. For ChatGPT, focus on entity consistency across authoritative sources.

Do not try to trick them. They are getting smarter. Be obvious. Be consistent. Be visible where it matters.

The exact Shopify schema that makes AI read your products

The two JSON-LD blocks every Shopify store needs so AI search can read who you are and what you sell — a Person/Organization identity block and a Product/Offer block.

Last updated: June 21, 2026

You need two JSON-LD blocks. First, a Person or Organization block with knowsAbout and hasOccupation to define who you are. Second, a Product block with Offer, price, and reviewCount to define what you sell. This explicit structure is the most reliable way to stop AI from guessing — or mislabeling — your business.

Why does AI mislabel my D2C brand?

I learned this the hard way. I used to think content was enough — great blogs, a strong story. It didn't work. AI search engines saw "Yauhen Massalski" and "basketball" and ignored my consulting work entirely. I was invisible to the clients who actually pay me.

I fixed it with structured data. Not keywords. Not backlinks. Code. Here's the exact pattern I used on yauhenm.com — clear instructions for a machine, nothing magic.

What schema do I need for my store?

Two blocks: one for your identity, one for your products.

1. The identity block (Person / Organization) — tells AI who owns the site. Founder-operator → use Person. Agency → use Organization.

  • name, url
  • hasOccupation: list both roles honestly — e.g. "Pro basketball player" and "AI-search & growth consultant." This bridges who you are.
  • knowsAbout: your expertise — "AI search," "SEO," "D2C growth."
  • makesOffer: link your identity to the service you sell.

2. The product block (Product / Offer) — tells AI what you sell. Don't rely on plain text; AI needs structure to extract price and availability.

  • @type: Product, name, image, description
  • offers: nested → price, priceCurrency, availability (e.g. https://schema.org/InStock)
  • aggregateRating (optional but powerful): reviewCount + ratingValue — only if the reviews are real.

Does schema alone get me cited?

No. Schema is necessary, not sufficient. It hands the AI the right answer to copy — but it doesn't make the AI care enough to cite you. You still need relevance and authority. Without schema, though, you're asking the AI to read your mind, and it usually guesses wrong.

Where does this go on Shopify?

Pick one approach, don't mix them:

  1. Theme editor (Online Store 2.0): most modern themes (Dawn, Prestige) let you add JSON-LD via the editor or a metafield.
  2. An app like "JSON-LD for SEO" — auto-generates the code. Easy, less control.
  3. Manual: edit theme.liquid, add the script in <head>. My preference — full control, no bloat.

Which fields actually matter?

Field Purpose Example
@type The entity Person or Product
name The label "Yauhen Massalski" / a product title
hasOccupation Your role(s) Pro athlete + consultant
knowsAbout Your expertise AI search, D2C growth
offers Sales data nested price / availability
availability In stock? schema.org/InStock
reviewCount Social proof only if real

How do I test it?

Never trust it blind. Use Google's free Rich Results Test: paste your URL or code, check for errors. No errors → AI can read it. Warnings → fix them. Nothing detected → you have nothing.

One honest caveat: schema getting read isn't the same as getting cited, and a citation isn't a sale. Cited ≠ clicks ≠ revenue. But it's the floor — skip it and you're invisible to the machines.

FAQ

What schema type does a Shopify store need?

Two: a Person or Organization identity block (knowsAbout, hasOccupation, makesOffer) and a Product/Offer block (price, availability, and aggregateRating if reviews are real).

How I run two supplement brands with almost no team

Running GMMY and YUMM requires AI to handle content pipelines and competitor intel, but humans must own strategy and quality control.

You run an ecommerce brand with a tiny team by automating the repetitive heavy lifting—content drafting, basic data sorting, and initial research—and keeping humans strictly for strategy, quality control, and high-touch customer relationships. I use AI to turn raw inputs into publishable drafts for my brands GMMY and YUMM, reducing operational drag so I can focus on growth decisions rather than production tasks.

I used to think "lean" meant starving myself of help until I broke. That was a mistake. It’s not about having no team. It’s about knowing what actually requires human judgment.

When I launched GMMY and later scaled YUMM, two separate Shopify supplement brands, I realized something uncomfortable. The more successful they got, the more administrative noise crept in. Emails, competitor price checks, blog outline generation, product description variations. It wasn’t growth work. It was maintenance.

I couldn’t hire a full team. Not yet. The margins didn’t support it, and hiring the wrong person is expensive. So I stopped trying to do it all manually. I built a system where AI does the heavy lifting. I do the editing.

This isn’t magic. It’s just ruthless prioritization. Here is exactly how I structure that workflow without losing my mind or my brand voice.

What can AI actually run in ecommerce?

AI is not a strategy consultant. It doesn’t know if your product fits the market today. But it is incredibly fast at processing information you already have.

I use AI for three things:

  1. Content generation: Turning raw ideas into drafts.
  2. Data synthesis: Summarizing competitor moves or regulatory updates.
  3. Repurposing: Taking one piece of long-form content and breaking it into emails, social posts, and blog snippets.

What I don’t let AI run: Customer support escalations, final pricing decisions, or creative direction. Those require taste and empathy. AI has neither.

How do you handle two brands without duplicating work?

Running GMMY and YUMM simultaneously would have killed me if I treated them as separate silos. They aren’t. They share a central nervous system: my content pipeline.

I don’t write twice. I create once, adapt everywhere. The workflow looks like this:

  1. Input: I record a voice note or draft a rough thought about a new product angle or industry trend.
  2. AI Processing: I feed that raw text into the AI with specific prompts for each brand’s tone (GMMY is clinical; YUMM is lifestyle).
  3. Output: I get two distinct drafts in minutes.
  4. Human Review: I edit for accuracy and brand voice. This takes 5 minutes, not 2 hours.

The tooling I built for competitor intel works the same way. Instead of manually checking rival sites daily, I have scripts pull pricing and stock changes. AI summarizes the deltas. If a competitor drops price by 10%, I see it in a digest. I decide if I care. The AI doesn’t decide for me.

Does this scale to more than two brands?

Yes, but with diminishing returns on your personal attention.

The system scales because the process is automated. The bottleneck is never the content generation. It’s your ability to review and approve.

If you add a third brand, you don’t need more writers. You need better templates. Standardize your prompts. Standardize your output format. If every piece of content looks like it came from the same factory floor, you can manage 10 brands as easily as two.

The danger is losing brand identity. When AI writes for GMMY and YUMM, it risks blending them together. That’s why the human review step is non-negotiable. I check every draft. Not because I don’t trust the AI, but because I trust my taste more.

What tools do you use?

I keep the stack minimal. Complexity kills execution.

  • Large Language Model: For drafting and summarizing. I use different system prompts for each brand to enforce tone separation.
  • Shopify App Stack: For basic automation of tags, metafields, and inventory alerts.
  • Custom Scripts: For pulling competitor data. I built these myself because existing tools were too generic.

I don’t buy expensive "AI marketing suites." They usually just wrap the same LLM API with a pretty dashboard. I write my own prompts. I control the inputs. If you rely on a third-party tool to "do your SEO," you’re already behind. You need to understand the mechanics, not just press buttons.

What still needs a human?

Strategy. Always strategy.

AI can tell you what was popular last month. It cannot tell you what will be popular next quarter in the supplement space. That requires intuition, market sensing, and risk tolerance.

Also, quality control. Supplements are health-adjacent. One hallucinated ingredient claim or dosage error can destroy a brand’s reputation overnight. I review every piece of medical or factual content. The AI handles the words; I handle the truth.

The one thing getting this wrong costs you

Most founders try to automate their way out of thinking. They want AI to "figure it out." It won’t. It will figure out what you ask it, poorly.

The win comes from clarity. If you can’t articulate your brand voice in a paragraph, no prompt will save you. Start there. Define the tone. Define the facts. Then let the machine do the typing.

No. It can handle basic FAQs like shipping times or return policies. But it cannot handle angry customers with complex issues. That requires empathy and discretion.

Do backlinks still matter for AI search?

Why Is My Shopify Store Invisible to ChatGPT?

Your store is likely invisible because a Cloudflare "Block AI Bots" toggle or aggressive WAF rule is hiding you from LLM crawlers. Fix your robots.txt and bot settings, not your content.

Your Shopify store is likely invisible to ChatGPT because a Cloudflare "Block AI Bots" toggle or an aggressive Web Application Firewall (WAF) rule is hiding you from Large Language Model crawlers. You can have perfect content, but if you block the bots that read it, AI models cannot index your site. The fix is simple: update your robots.txt to allow specific AI user-agents and verify your Cloudflare bot management settings are not blocking them.

I used to think visibility was about content quality. I was wrong.

Early in my consulting career, I worked with a specialty eyewear brand. Their product pages were beautiful. The copy was crisp. They had backlinks from major publications. Yet, when I asked ChatGPT about niche sunglasses, their brand didn’t show up. Not even in the "I couldn't find results" fallback.

I assumed their SEO was weak. I was ready to write a 3,000-word guide on semantic keywords.

Then I ran a technical audit. The problem wasn’t the words. It was the gatekeeper.

They had enabled a "Block AI Bots" feature in Cloudflare’s bot management suite. It was designed to stop scrapers and copyright thieves. It worked too well. It also stopped ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Google from seeing their site.

Content doesn’t matter if no one can read it.

Here is the blunt truth about AI visibility for D2C founders.

Why is my Shopify store invisible to ChatGPT?

The most common cause of invisibility is technical blocking, not content quality.

AI models like ChatGPT (via OAI-SearchBot) and Perplexity (via PerplexityBot) rely on public web access. If you block these specific user-agents at the server or CDN level, they cannot crawl your pages. Your site becomes a ghost town to them.

This is different from traditional SEO. Googlebot is allowed almost everywhere. But AI crawlers are often treated as "high-risk" traffic by security tools. You might have inadvertently blocked yourself while trying to protect your business.

How do I know if I'm blocking AI bots?

You need to check two places: your robots.txt file and your Cloudflare (or other CDN) settings.

First, look at your robots.txt. This is the first thing any bot checks. If it says Disallow: /, you are blocked.

Second, check your WAF rules. Many Shopify apps or manual Cloudflare configurations add rules to block "bad bots." These lists often include AI crawlers by mistake.

Here is a safe configuration for your robots.txt. It allows the major search engines and the leading AI models. Place this at the root of your domain (e.g., yoursite.com/robots.txt).

User-agent: *
Allow: /

# Allow Google Search Bot
User-agent: Googlebot
Allow: /

# Allow Bing Search Bot
User-agent: Bingbot
Allow: /

# Allow ChatGPT Web Browser (OAI-SearchBot)
User-agent: OAI-SearchBot
Allow: /

# Allow Perplexity AI
User-agent: PerplexityBot
Allow: /

# Allow Anthropic Claude (if applicable)
User-agent: ClaudeBot
Allow: /

# Block specific scrapers if needed
User-agent: SemrushBot
Disallow: /
User-agent: AhrefsBot
Disallow: /

Note: Always verify the current user-agent strings. Bot names can change as models update their infrastructure. The ones listed above are the standard targets for D2C visibility in 2026.

Training bots vs. search bots?

There is a distinction that confuses many founders.

  1. Search Bots: These crawl the web to answer user queries. Examples: Googlebot, Bingbot, OAI-SearchBot, PerplexityBot. You WANT these to access your site.
  2. Training Bots: These are used by companies to build their models from scratch. They often ignore robots.txt or operate on different infrastructure.

You cannot control training bots directly via your website settings. You can only control search bots. By allowing the search bots, you increase the likelihood that your content is ingested into the models' context windows during updates.

If you block OAI-SearchBot, ChatGPT cannot cite your site in real-time. Your brand remains invisible to its live search feature.

Does this affect Google?

Usually, no.

Googlebot and Bingbot are distinct user-agents from OAI-SearchBot. Blocking AI bots typically does not hurt your traditional Google rankings. However, the reverse is true: if you block Googlebot to save server costs or stop scrapers, you will kill your organic traffic entirely.

Always keep Googlebot allowed. Never use "Block All" rules unless you are building a private intranet.

How do I unblock safely?

Do not just delete all bot rules. That is dangerous.

  1. Audit your robots.txt: Ensure the lines above are present. Use a robots.txt validator tool to check for syntax errors.
  2. Check Cloudflare Bot Management: Go to your Cloudflare dashboard. Look for "Bot Fight Mode" or "AI Bot Challenge." Ensure OAI-SearchBot and PerplexityBot are not in the blocked list. You may need to add them to an "Allow" list.
  3. Check Shopify Apps: Some privacy-focused apps (like certain cookie consent or anti-scraper tools) inject JavaScript blocks. Test your site with a "User-Agent Switcher" browser extension. Set your browser to OAI-SearchBot and try to load your homepage. If it loads, you are good. If it shows an error or blank page, something is blocking you.
  4. Wait for Re-crawl: After making changes, give it 24-48 hours. AI models do not crawl in real-time like humans. Patience is part of the fix.

Is this a quick fix?

Yes and no. The technical change takes five minutes. The visibility gain takes time — make the changes, then re-check in a couple of days, because AI crawlers don't re-read your site in real time.

The Merchant Feed Gap: Why Your Products Are Invisible to AI

Most stores are invisible to AI shopping answers because their product feed skips review_count, ratings, and GTINs — and the ChatGPT/Perplexity merchant programs. Here's the fix.

You get products into AI shopping answers by enabling the ChatGPT Merchant Program on Shopify, joining Perplexity’s free merchant program, and enriching your feed with review_count, average_rating, GTIN, and live price/stock data. Without these fields, AI models have no structured proof to cite.

Why do most stores miss this?

I worked with a specialty eyewear brand last year. They had great margins. Great product. But they were invisible to every new search engine.

They thought SEO was just meta tags. It isn’t anymore.

AI doesn’t crawl your homepage like Google did in 2015. It pulls from structured data feeds. If you don’t speak that language, you don’t exist.

Most merchants ship product feeds with no review count or rating. They skip the ChatGPT and Perplexity merchant programs entirely. This is the gap.

You can have the best brand in the world. If your feed lacks review_count, AI won’t trust you enough to recommend you. It needs proof points.

What fields actually matter?

AI models are risk-averse. They don’t want to recommend a bad product. They need signals that you are legit.

Here is what they look for:

Field Why it matters
review_count Social proof volume. Zero = suspicious.
average_rating Quality signal. Needs to be 4.0+.
GTIN / Brand Identity. Matches global databases.
Live Price/Stock Relevance. AI hates outdated info.

If your feed is missing these, you are flying blind.

Which programs should you join?

Two programs matter right now.

  1. ChatGPT Merchant Program: If you use Shopify, your catalog auto-integrates. You just need to enable it. It pulls directly from your store’s backend.
  2. Perplexity Free Merchant Program: This is free. It lets Perplexity cite your products in its search answers.

Do both. They cover the two biggest AI traffic sources.

Does Shopify sync automatically?

Yes, but only if you turn it on.

Shopify has a built-in integration for ChatGPT. You go to Settings > Apps and Sales Channels. Find the ChatGPT section. Enable the connection.

It pulls your product title, price, image, and availability. But it does not pull your reviews unless you have an app that pushes that data into the feed structure.

You need to check your feed output. Make sure review_count is present. If it is zero, AI ignores you.

How do I fix my feed?

Start with the basics.

  1. Audit your current feed. Look at the raw XML or CSV.
  2. Check for GTINs. Every product needs a Global Trade Item Number. No GTIN = no global recognition.
  3. Add review data. Use an app like Judge.me or Yotpo. Ensure they push average_rating and review_count to your feed.
  4. Enable programs. Turn on ChatGPT Merchant Program. Apply for Perplexity’s program.

This takes one afternoon. The impact lasts years.

Is this just for big brands?

No. I see small D2C brands getting cited because they have better data than the giants.

Big brands often have messy feeds. Small brands can be clean. Clean data wins in AI search.

AI loves structure. It hates ambiguity. Give it structure, and it will give you visibility.

Can I do this myself?

Yes. You don’t need a coder. You need an ops mindset.

Check your feed daily. Watch for drops in review_count. Ensure prices are live. If your stock is out of sync, AI will recommend dead links. That kills trust fast.
